The Process

How collaborative divorce works.

A structured, step-by-step process — each phase designed to keep the focus on resolution and cooperation, with your family at the center.

Step 01

Separate initial consultations

Each spouse meets individually with a collaboratively trained attorney. These confidential meetings allow each person to understand the process, ask questions, and decide whether a collaborative approach fits their situation — with no obligation to proceed.

The attorney explains the Participation Agreement, reviews what topics will need to be addressed — property, finances, support, parenting — and helps the client think through their goals before any joint meetings begin.

Step 02

Signing the Participation Agreement

Both spouses and both attorneys sign a Participation Agreement — the foundational document that commits everyone to the collaborative process. It establishes the ground rules: full voluntary disclosure of information, respectful communication, and a shared commitment to reaching a negotiated resolution.

Crucially, the agreement states that if either party abandons the process and chooses to litigate, both attorneys must withdraw. This creates a strong mutual incentive to stay the course.

Why this matters

"The withdrawal provision ensures attorneys remain settlement-focused throughout, because their role ends the moment the process does."

Step 03

Building the team

Depending on your situation, neutral specialists join the process. A financial specialist gathers and analyzes all relevant financial data. If children are involved, a child specialist helps parents understand their children's needs. A divorce coach supports both spouses in managing the emotional and communication challenges of the process.

These professionals work as neutrals — serving both parties equally. You share the cost of one expert per discipline rather than each side retaining their own, keeping costs down and the focus on solutions.

Step 04

Joint four-way sessions

Both spouses and both attorneys meet together in a series of structured sessions. Information is shared openly, options are generated, and agreements are negotiated — at your pace, not a court calendar's.

Sessions are typically organized by topic: financial disclosures, then property division, support arrangements, and parenting plans. There is no judge, no court reporter, and no adversarial maneuvering. The sessions are entirely solution-focused.

Step 05

Drafting the settlement agreement

Once both parties have reached agreement on all outstanding issues, the attorneys draft a comprehensive settlement agreement reflecting every decision made. Both spouses review the document with their respective attorneys before signing.

This document addresses all legal requirements: division of assets and debts, spousal support if applicable, and a parenting plan outlining custody, parenting time, and decision-making for any children involved.

Step 06

Court filing and finalization

The signed settlement agreement is submitted to the court as an uncontested divorce. Because everything is already agreed upon, the process is straightforward and typically requires no hearing.

You leave with an agreement you helped create — one tailored to your family's actual circumstances, not a generic ruling imposed from the bench.

For Clarity

What it isn't.

Collaborative divorce is sometimes confused with other processes. Understanding the distinctions helps you make a more informed choice.

Collaborative divorce is not mediation. In mediation, a single neutral helps both parties reach agreement, but neither person has their own attorney at the table. In collaborative divorce, each spouse has their own attorney throughout every session — within a cooperative, rather than adversarial, framework.

It is not a DIY divorce. Both parties retain trained legal counsel. The difference is that the attorneys work collaboratively alongside neutral specialists, rather than against each other.

It is not appropriate for every situation. Cases involving domestic violence, significant power imbalances, or active substance abuse may call for different approaches. An honest initial consultation will help you determine whether collaborative is right for your family.

Timeline

What to expect.

Every family's situation is different, but collaborative divorce typically moves faster than litigation — and the pace is yours to set.

Straightforward cases

3 – 6 months

Couples with relatively clear finances, no children, or well-aligned parenting goals often complete the process in under six months.

Moderate complexity

6 – 12 months

Cases involving real estate, retirement accounts, business interests, or parenting questions typically take six to twelve months.

High complexity

12+ months

Significant assets, business valuations, or particularly complex dynamics can extend the timeline — still typically shorter than contested litigation.
