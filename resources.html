The Professionals

Role of each professional.

Role of the Attorneys

At the outset of a collaborative divorce, both spouses retain their own collaboratively trained attorneys. It is important for both parties to have their own attorney because one attorney cannot ethically represent or advise both sides in a divorce.

Key functions include: providing independent legal guidance; drafting and advising on the Participation Agreement; conducting structured four-way negotiations; and coordinating with other team members — divorce coaches, child specialists, and financial specialists — to address all dimensions of the case efficiently.

In short, collaborative divorce attorneys guide and support their clients through a structured, team-based process designed to reach fair and durable agreements without the need for litigation.

Role of the Financial Specialist

The financial specialist is a trained collaborative divorce expert who serves as a neutral, unbiased financial professional supporting both spouses and their attorneys. They provide objective analysis and financial insight to help the parties reach well-informed solutions. Generally, the parties work with one financial specialist — instead of two experts in a court-focused case — thereby avoiding the costs of duplication.

Functions include: collecting and organizing all financial information (investments, retirement accounts, business interests, real property, and debt); analyzing income, expenses, and cash flow; developing and evaluating financial settlement scenarios with projections; and providing tax information to help both spouses avoid unintended consequences. If needed, the team may also consult mortgage loan officers, realtors, business valuators, and CPAs.

Role of the Divorce Coach

In the collaborative divorce process, a divorce coach is a neutral mental-health professional who helps both spouses manage the emotional and communication side of the divorce. The coach does not provide therapy and does not give legal advice, but supports and promotes productive communications, makes sure everyone is heard, and keeps the process on track. Generally, the parties work with one coach who assists both, together or separately.

Key roles include: improving communication between spouses; helping manage anger, fear, or grief so emotions do not derail negotiations; keeping meetings focused and productive; supporting co-parenting conversations when children are involved; preparing clients for important conversations; and addressing emotional roadblocks early to protect the process.

Role of the Child Specialist

The collaborative divorce child specialist is a licensed mental health professional, typically a psychologist or social worker. The child specialist acts as a voice for the children during collaborative discussions, focusing on the children's unique needs and best interests within their specific family. The child specialist does not act as an evaluator or therapist.

Key roles include: helping parents appreciate their children's emotions and age-appropriate needs; assisting in crafting tailor-made parenting plans specific to the family — instead of generic court schedules; consulting with other professionals involved with the children such as teachers or therapists; and, where helpful and requested by both parents, meeting directly with the children. Working with one child specialist — instead of two competing experts — reduces costs and promotes better long-term outcomes.
