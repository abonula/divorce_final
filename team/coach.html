The Divorce Coach.

A neutral mental-health professional who helps both spouses manage the emotional and communication side of the divorce — keeping the process on track and moving forward.

The Role

What they do.

In the collaborative divorce process, a divorce coach is a neutral mental-health professional who helps both spouses manage the emotional and communication side of the divorce. The coach does not provide therapy and does not give legal advice, but supports and promotes productive communications, makes sure everyone is heard, and keeps the process on track. Generally, the parties work with one coach who assists both, together or separately.

  • Improving communication: helping spouses express concerns clearly and listen without escalating conflict
  • Managing emotions: assisting parties in dealing with anger, fear, or grief so emotions do not derail negotiations
  • Keeping meetings productive: setting agendas, reducing tension, and guiding conversations toward problem-solving
  • Supporting co-parenting: when children are involved, helping parents focus on the children's needs and build solid parenting plans
  • Preparing clients for meetings: often meeting individually with each spouse to prepare for important conversations
  • Protecting the collaborative process: addressing emotional roadblocks early to prevent breakdowns that could push the case into litigation

In short, the divorce coach acts as an emotional and communication guide, helping both spouses work together respectfully and efficiently toward a settlement.

The Advantage

One coach, shared benefit.

In a collaborative divorce, both spouses work with one neutral divorce coach — rather than each retaining their own mental health expert. This keeps costs down, promotes genuine communication between the parties, and ensures that the coach's goal is always the well-being of the process itself, not advocacy for either side.
