Attorneys

At the outset of a collaborative divorce, both spouses retain their own collaboratively trained attorneys. It is important for both parties to have their own attorney because one attorney cannot ethically represent or advise both sides in a divorce.

Collaborative attorneys provide legal guidance, draft and advise on the Participation Agreement, coordinate structured four-way meetings, and guide their respective client through the full process — with resolution as the goal.

Financial Specialists

The financial specialist is a trained collaborative divorce expert who serves as a neutral, unbiased financial professional supporting both spouses and their attorneys. Generally, the parties work with one financial specialist — instead of two experts in a court-focused case — avoiding the costs of duplication.

The financial specialist assists the parties in developing settlements that are equitable, sustainable, and aligned with both parties' financial goals.

Divorce Coaches

In the collaborative divorce process, a divorce coach is a neutral mental-health professional who helps both spouses manage the emotional and communication side of the divorce. The coach does not provide therapy and does not give legal advice, but supports productive communications, makes sure everyone is heard, and keeps the process on track.

Child Specialists

The collaborative divorce child specialist is a licensed mental health professional, typically a psychologist or social worker. The child specialist acts as a voice for the children during collaborative discussions, focusing on the children's unique needs and best interests. The child specialist does not act as an evaluator or therapist.

The parties work with one child specialist — instead of two custody experts — helping to promote satisfactory post-divorce outcomes with a much reduced risk of further disputes.
